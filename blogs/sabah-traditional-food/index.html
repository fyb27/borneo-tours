Sabah Traditional Food: The Dishes Worth Travelling For

July 12, 2026

Sabah's food story is usually told from the coast: seafood dinners in Kota Kinabalu, the famous tuaran mee, weekend crowds at Gaya Street. All excellent. But the older story is inland, in farm kitchens and longhouses where dishes were shaped by the hill rice cycle, the forest and the river. Here is what traditional Sabahan food actually means, dish by dish.

1. Hinava

The signature. Fresh fish, usually mackerel, cured raw in lime juice with chilli, shallots, ginger and grated bambangan seed. Sharp, clean and addictive; if Sabah had a national dish, this is it.

2. Linopot

Hill rice packed warm into a broad doringin or tarap leaf, folded into a neat parcel that travels to the farm and back. Linopot is the interior's ancient lunchbox, and the leaf lends the rice a faint fragrance no pot can copy. Often the rice is mixed with mashed sweet potato or hill varieties in shades of purple and red. Once purely practical, it is now served with pride at festivals and village meals.

3. Sinalau bakas

The Murut masterpiece: wild boar slow-smoked over hardwood at roadside stalls across the interior, especially along the Keningau and Nabawan roads. Dark, dense, deeply savoury, and eaten with rice and a fierce chilli dip. Non-halal, gloriously so.

4. Tapai

Rice wine, fermented in ceramic jars and drunk through bamboo straws, the drink of every celebration in the interior. The Murut version is a ritual of hospitality as much as a beverage; read the full story in our tapai guide.

5. Bambangan

A wild jungle mango with a scent you will not forget, most loved pickled: the flesh fermented with salt and its own grated seed. Served alongside rice and grilled fish or meat, it does the job pickles do everywhere, only louder.

6. Tuaran mee

From the town of Tuaran near Kota Kinabalu: springy handmade egg noodles wok-fried until fragrant, classically with egg and char siu or, in the non-halal originals, crisp roast pork. Simple, perfect, worth the detour.

7. Pinasakan

An everyday Kadazan-Dusun classic: fish braised slowly with sour jungle fruit and turmeric until the sauce turns sharp and golden. Village comfort food, made for rice.

8. Butod

The dare on the list: sago palm grubs, eaten live by the brave or fried until golden for everyone else. Fried, they honestly taste like smoky, creamy crackling. At tamu markets you will find them sold by the scoop.

9. Latok

From the coast: sea grapes, strands of tiny seaweed pearls that pop between the teeth, usually eaten near-raw with lime and chilli. The east coast markets around Semporna do them best.

10. Hill rice itself

It sounds too plain to list, but Sabah's interior rice, grown dry on hillsides in varieties handed down for generations, is a different food from lowland white rice: nuttier, firmer, worth eating attention. At harvest time it becomes the centre of Kaamatan, the state's biggest festival.

Where to eat all this

Tamu weekly markets are the great showcase: every district has one, piled with bambangan jars, butod, jungle ferns and whatever the forest offered that week. Gaya Street in Kota Kinabalu works on Sundays. But the real thing is a village kitchen. On community-hosted journeys in Orou Sapulot, dishes like linopot, pinasakan, fresh jungle fern and smoked bakas are simply what dinner is, cooked by the families who grew, caught or smoked it themselves.

Frequently Asked Questions

What food is Sabah famous for? Hinava (lime-cured raw fish), sinalau bakas (Murut smoked wild boar), linopot (leaf-wrapped hill rice), bambangan (pickled wild mango), tuaran mee, butod (sago grubs) and tapai rice wine are the classics, alongside exceptional seafood on the coast.

What is linopot? Linopot is hill rice packed warm into a broad leaf, traditionally the doringin or tarap leaf, so it folds into a portable parcel. It was the farmer's packed lunch of Sabah's interior and is enjoying a proud revival at cultural events and village tables.

What is hinava? Hinava is Sabah's best-known Kadazan-Dusun dish: fresh raw fish cured in lime juice with chilli, shallots, ginger and shaved bambangan seed. Think of it as Borneo's answer to ceviche, sharp and clean.

Where can I try traditional Sabahan food? Weekly tamu markets across the state, the Gaya Street Sunday market in Kota Kinabalu, and above all village kitchens. Community-based stays in the interior, such as those in Sapulot, serve traditional dishes as everyday food rather than restaurant novelties.

Eat this food where it is grown, on a community-hosted journey to Orou Sapulot.
