Borneo Food: What to Actually Eat, Coast to Interior

July 8, 2026

Eating your way across Borneo means eating across three countries, dozens of indigenous cuisines, and every possible distance from the sea. The good news: it is one of Southeast Asia's most underrated food destinations precisely because nobody told the crowds. Here is the map, from famous bowls to longhouse tables.

The famous bowls

Kuching's Sarawak laksa is the celebrity: a tangled bowl of rice vermicelli in a broth that splits the difference between curry and prawn stock, finished with lime and sambal. Its rival kolo mee, springy noodles tossed in shallot oil, is what Kuching actually eats for breakfast. Over in Sabah, tuaran mee, wok-kissed handmade egg noodles, holds the same throne.

The coast: seafood without ceremony

Both Malaysian states eat seafood at a level that embarrasses most capitals, at market prices. Grilled fish with lime and sambal, buttered prawns, and in Sabah the raw classic hinava, lime-cured mackerel sharpened with chilli and wild mango seed. The seafood night markets of Kota Kinabalu and the east coast towns are dinner solved.

The jungle vegetables

Borneo's green signature is midin, a crisp jungle fern stir-fried with garlic or belacan, joined in Sabah by the similar pakis. Sweet, snappy and served everywhere from city cafes to longhouses; order it the moment you see it. Wild bambangan mango, pickled with its own grated seed, does the sharp work alongside.

The interior: longhouse cooking

Inland, food becomes what the hill farm, the river and the forest provide, and this is where Borneo eating gets memorable. Hill rice, nuttier and firmer than lowland grain, sometimes arriving wrapped in a leaf as linopot. River fish grilled or braised sour. Jungle ferns picked that afternoon. Bamboo used as the cooking pot itself, chicken and rice steamed inside a green tube over the fire. And in Murut country, the king of interior proteins: sinalau bakas, wild boar smoked dark over hardwood at roadside stalls across Sabah's interior.

Meals like this are not restaurant food; they are hospitality. At community-hosted stays like Orou Sapulot, dinner is cooked by the longhouse families themselves, and eating it, everyone cross-legged, dishes shared, is as much the experience as anything on the itinerary.

The drinks

Borneo's real drink is rice wine. Sabah ferments tapai in ceramic jars drunk through bamboo straws, plus the sweeter lihing; Sarawak pours tuak at every Iban celebration. Refusing a first sip takes more diplomacy than accepting it. Beyond that: strong local coffee in every kedai, fresh coconut and calamansi juice, and heavily taxed but available beer.

The brave list

For collectors: butod sago grubs (fried, they taste like smoky crackling), latok sea grapes popping like caviar, durian in season, and jars of fermented everything at the weekly tamu markets. None obligatory, all memorable.

How to eat well in Borneo

Follow three rules. Eat where it is crowded at the local mealtime. Treat every tamu market as a food museum with samples. And take any invitation to eat in a village; the island's best kitchen has always been the longhouse.

Frequently Asked Questions

What food is Borneo known for? Sarawak laksa and kolo mee in the west; hinava, tuaran mee and smoked wild boar in Sabah; midin jungle fern everywhere it grows; fresh seafood on every coast; and in the interior, longhouse cooking built on hill rice, river fish and the forest.

Is food in Borneo spicy? Milder than Thailand, gentler than Sumatra. Chilli usually arrives as a side sambal you control yourself, so cautious eaters and children manage easily, while chilli lovers can escalate at will.

Is it easy to eat vegetarian in Borneo? In cities, yes: Chinese and Indian kitchens offer plenty. In villages it takes a heads-up, but rice, jungle ferns, eggs and garden vegetables are staples anyway, and hosts adapt happily when told in advance.

Can you drink alcohol in Borneo? Yes. Malaysia taxes alcohol heavily so bars are pricey, but Borneo's indigenous communities brew their own: tapai and lihing rice wines in Sabah, tuak in Sarawak, poured generously at any celebration.

The best meal in Borneo is the one cooked for you in a longhouse: find it at Orou Sapulot.
