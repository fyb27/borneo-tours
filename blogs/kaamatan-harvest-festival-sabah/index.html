Kaamatan: Sabah's Harvest Festival, Explained by Locals

July 13, 2026

Every May, Sabah slows down its ordinary business and turns to its oldest one: the rice harvest. Pesta Kaamatan, the harvest festival, fills the month with cultural nights, buffalo-strength arm wrestling, beauty pageants, bamboo music and a truly heroic quantity of rice wine, before peaking in the two-day state holiday on 30 and 31 May. If you want to understand Sabah, Kaamatan is the state explaining itself.

What Kaamatan is about

At its heart, Kaamatan is thanksgiving. In Kadazan-Dusun belief, rice is not just a crop: it carries the spirit of Huminodun, the maiden sacrificed by her father so that her people could eat. The harvest festival honours that gift. The rituals of the season, led by traditional priestesses called bobohizan, centre on gathering and honouring the rice spirit so the next planting will thrive.

Around that sacred core has grown a joyous, very public month. Villages hold their own kaamatan; districts hold bigger ones; and everything converges at the end of May on the KDCA cultural grounds in Penampang, outside Kota Kinabalu, for the state finals of everything.

What actually happens

Music and dance, first of all: gong ensembles, the sumazau dance with its slow outstretched arms, and cultural troupes from every district. Sports with roots in farm life, like arm wrestling and blowpipe shooting. The Unduk Ngadau pageant, in which young women representing their districts compete in traditional dress, honouring Huminodun herself. Sugandoi singing contests. And everywhere, food stalls and open jars of tapai and lihing, the rice wines without which no harvest was ever properly celebrated.

The Murut and the harvest

Kaamatan's public face is Kadazan-Dusun, but Sabah's other indigenous communities celebrate the harvest just as seriously. For the Murut people of the interior, the season's spirit lives in the longhouse: jars of tapai opened for guests, the magunatip bamboo dance clacking late into the night, and hill rice at the centre of every plate. The Murut also have their own showcase festival, Pesta Kalimaran, held at the Murut Cultural Centre near Tenom, celebrating Murut craft, dress, music and language.

Visiting during Kaamatan

If your trip lands in May, go. The KDCA grounds on 30 and 31 May are open to all and cheerfully overwhelming; district celebrations earlier in the month are smaller and often more personal. Dress for heat, accept the tapai straw when it is offered to you, and do not plan anything ambitious for the morning after.

And if you miss May entirely, the culture Kaamatan celebrates does not close for the year. In Murut villages around Orou Sapulot, the longhouse evening, the rice, the tapai jar and the bamboo poles are part of ordinary hospitality in every month, harvest or not.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Pesta Kaamatan? Kaamatan is Sabah's harvest festival, held throughout May and peaking on 30 and 31 May, which are public holidays in Sabah. It gives thanks for the rice harvest and is the biggest cultural celebration of the state's indigenous communities.

Who celebrates Kaamatan? It is rooted in Kadazan-Dusun tradition, but in practice all of Sabah joins in, including the Murut, whose own harvest-linked celebrations centre on tapai, music and the longhouse. Villages, districts and workplaces across the state hold their own events through May.

What is the Unduk Ngadau? The Unduk Ngadau is Kaamatan's famous cultural pageant. It honours Huminodun, the maiden of Kadazan-Dusun legend whose sacrifice gave rice to the people, and district winners compete at the state finals at the end of May.

Can tourists attend Kaamatan? Yes. The state-level celebrations at the KDCA grounds in Penampang near Kota Kinabalu on 30 and 31 May are open to everyone, and district events through May are welcoming. Expect traditional dress, dance, sports, endless food and generous jars of tapai.

Experience harvest culture, tapai and longhouse hospitality year-round at Orou Sapulot in the Murut heartland.
